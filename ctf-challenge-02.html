I. Gaius Julius Caesar — A Man of Numbers
Gaius Julius Caesar was, by all historical accounts, a man of remarkable discipline and obsessive habit. His Commentarii de Bello Gallico reveals not only a brilliant military strategist but also a mind deeply attached to symbolic numerology. Roman historians — most notably Suetonius — note that Caesar held a particular fondness for the number seven.

This attachment was not merely superstitious. Caesar organised his legions in groups of seven cohorts for key engagements. He scheduled his most critical campaigns to begin on the 7th day of the month. Even the famous Veni, vidi, vici — three words, but 7 syllables. Coincidence? His biographers thought not.
II. The Cipher
It was during the Gallic campaigns that Caesar first employed what we now call the Caesar Cipher — a simple substitution method in which each letter of the alphabet is shifted by a fixed number of positions. Messages dispatched to generals in the field were encoded; only those who knew the shift value could read them.

According to Suetonius, Caesar typically used a shift of three for routine correspondence. But for his most sensitive dispatches — those containing troop movements, asset locations, and personal orders — he reserved a different value. One that held personal meaning. His biographer writes:
Suetonius · De Vita Caesarum · c. 121 AD
"...and for those letters which he wished no common soldier to decipher, he would advance each character not by three, but by as many places as his most favoured number — that number which he held sacred above all others, which he said the gods themselves had whispered to him on the eve of the Rubicon crossing. Seven, always seven."
Modern cryptographers classify this as a monoalphabetic substitution cipher — trivially broken today, yet sufficient for a world without computers. The irony, of course, is that Caesar considered it unbreakable.
